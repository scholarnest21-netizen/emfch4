Electromotive Force (EMF)

In 1831, Michael Faraday in England and independently Joseph Henry in the United States found experimentally that a change of magnetic field through a coil wire induces a current in the wire. The results of these experiments were the foundation of a new law of nature called Faraday's Law of Induction.

🔑 Key Concept Electromagnetic induction is the phenomenon that provides the link between electric and magnetic fields.

EMF and Battery

Positive charges are carried from lower potential to higher potential inside the battery. Some carrier inside the battery carries these charges against the electric (Coulomb) force. These may be chemical, nuclear forces, or even light energy — these are called non-electromagnetic agents acting inside the battery.

Mathematical Definition of EMF

The electromotive force is defined as the line integral of force per unit charge around a closed path:

Electromotive Force
\[\varepsilon = \oint_{\text{closed path}} \frac{\vec{F} \cdot d\vec{l}}{q}\]

This is very similar to electric potential. The potential difference between \(V_a\) and \(V_b\) (where \(V_a > V_b\)) is:

\[\int_A^B \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{S} = V_A - V_B\]
⚠ Key Distinction The units of both EMF and potential are Volts. However, in Faraday's law, the EMF is a non-zero closed integral — meaning it should not be treated as a potential gradient. The closed loop integral of electric potential is always zero (conservative field), but EMF is not.

The closed loop integral may become zero when we move around a loop from a positive gradient to a lower one. But in the context of Faraday's law, this electromotive force is non-zero:

\[\varepsilon = \oint_{\text{closed path}} \frac{\vec{F} \cdot d\vec{l}}{q}\]

If we have a wire along the path and if \(\varepsilon \neq 0\), then with charge \(q \neq 0\), current will flow. When the closed path is a wire and \(\varepsilon \neq 0\), the current that flows is given by Ohm's Law:

⭐ Ohm's Law form
\[\varepsilon = IR\]

The EMF can also be defined for a path through space — the closed loop integral gives the EMF around any closed arbitrary path, whether or not a wire is present. If a wire is present, current will flow.

Faraday's Law

In 1819, Oersted discovered that a steady current produces a steady magnetic field — connecting electricity with magnetism. A little later, Faraday suggested that perhaps a steady magnetic field could produce a steady current. He performed many experiments to test this hypothesis — and it turned out not to be so!

✅ Faraday's Conclusion A steady magnetic field (as produced by a solenoid) does NOT produce a steady current in a nearby loop. However, a changing magnetic field does cause a current. This phenomenon is called electromagnetic induction.

Faraday's Law — Statement

Faraday's Law
The EMF around a closed loop equals the negative of the time rate of change of magnetic flux through the surface bounded by that loop.
⭐ Faraday's Law (Integral Form)
\[\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi_{mag}}{dt}\]

Generalization of Faraday's Law

  • It doesn't matter who is changing — whether the loop is moving or the field is changing with time.
  • It doesn't matter whether a wire is present or not. The law is true for any arbitrary contour in the universe.
  • This law is not about wires and current — it is about the electric field and the changing magnetic field.
  • This is one of Maxwell's equations — relating the electric field to the time-varying magnetic field.
  • It cannot be derived from Gauss's, Coulomb's, or Ampere's laws — it is a fundamentally new law of nature.
📌 Important Note Faraday arrived at this law through numerous experiments and concluded it to be a general result. Nothing studied before (Gauss, Coulomb, Ampere) can be used to derive it.

General Integral Form

⭐ Faraday's Law — General Form
\[\oint_c \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = -\frac{d}{dt} \oint_s \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S}\]

Motional EMF

Motional EMF arises from the magnetic force on charges inside a conductor moving in a magnetic field.

Consider a rectangular conducting loop partially inside a magnetic field region. Charges \(q_1\) to \(q_4\) are all positive. The element carrying \(q_2\) is not placed in the magnetic field, hence the net force on it is zero.

Forces on segments (using Lorentz force)

Force on element 1 (bottom leg)
\[\vec{F}_{q_1} = qvB(-\hat{j})\]
Force on elements 3 and 4
\[\vec{F}_{q_3} = qvB(-\hat{j}) \qquad \vec{F}_{q_4} = qvB(-\hat{j})\]

The forces on \(q_1\) and \(q_3\) (horizontal legs) are perpendicular to the wire and therefore will not create any current. Only the vertical left leg contributes:

Motional EMF — General
\[\varepsilon = \oint_{\text{rectangle}} \frac{\vec{F}_{mag}}{q} \cdot d\vec{l}\]
Simplifying (only left leg contributes)
\[\varepsilon = \oint_{\text{left leg}} \frac{q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{l}}{q} = \oint_{\text{left leg}} (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{l}\]
⭐ Result
\[\boxed{\varepsilon = vBw}\] where \(w\) is the width of the loop (height of left leg in the field).

Deriving Faraday's Law from Motional EMF

Assume the loop has moved \(\Delta l\) in time \(\Delta t\). The change in flux is:

Change in flux (negative = flux decreasing)
\[\Delta\Phi_{mag} = -Bw\Delta l\]

The negative sign indicates a decrease in flux because we are moving the loop away from the magnetic field.

Rate of change of flux
\[\frac{\Delta\Phi_{mag}}{\Delta t} = -Bw\frac{\Delta l}{\Delta t} \implies \frac{d\Phi_{mag}}{dt} = -Bwv\]

Comparing with equation (1) for motional EMF (\(\varepsilon = vBw\)):

⭐ Faraday's Law Derived
\[\boxed{\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi_{mag}}{dt}}\]
✅ Key Insight This result is derived in one specific context (a moving rectangular loop), but Faraday's law is always true in more general contexts — any loop, any shape, any cause of changing flux.
4

Lenz's Law

Lenz's Law
The induced EMF is always in a direction so as to oppose the change in flux that causes it.

Lenz's Law gives the direction of the induced current. The negative sign in Faraday's law \(\varepsilon = -d\Phi/dt\) is the mathematical expression of Lenz's Law.

Three Cases of Changing Magnetic Flux

The magnetic flux through a surface can change with time due to one of the following:

Case 1: Moving Loop

The area of the loop changes with time — or the loop moves through a non-uniform B-field. This is motional EMF.

Case 2: Rotating Loop

The orientation of the surface with respect to the magnetic field changes with time. The loop rotates in a B-field.

Case 3: Changing B

The magnitude of the magnetic field changes with time (\(dB/dt \neq 0\)) while the loop remains stationary.

Faraday's Law and Induced Electric Field

In Cases 1 & 2, there is relative motion between the loop and field, producing motional EMF:

\[\varepsilon = \oint_{\text{loop}} \frac{\vec{F}_{mag}}{q} \cdot d\vec{l} = \oint_{\text{loop}} (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{l}\]

In Case 3, there is no relative motion. We cannot express EMF in terms of \(v\). Instead, a time-varying magnetic field produces an electric field in the circuit which causes the motion of charges:

\[\varepsilon = \oint_{\text{loop}} \frac{\vec{F}_{ele}}{q} \cdot d\vec{l} = \oint_{\text{loop}} \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l}\]
💡 Non-Conservative Electric Field In this case:
  • The line integral of the electric field over a closed loop is non-zero.
  • The electric field is non-conservative — due to time-varying magnetic flux.
  • The electric field lines (in this case) are closed loops — unlike electrostatic E-field lines which start/end on charges.

Electrostatic E-field (Conservative)

Source: static charges. E-lines are open — start at +q and end at −q (or at ∞). \(\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = 0\)

Induced E-field (Non-Conservative)

Source: time-dependent magnetic flux \(\vec{B}(t)\). E-lines are closed loops. \(\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} \neq 0\)

Inductance

The time-varying current produces a time-varying magnetic field which in turn produces an induced EMF. The magnetic flux \(\Phi\) is proportional to the current \(I\):

\[\Phi \propto I\]

The proportionality constant is called Inductance. There are two types:

1. Mutual Inductance (M)

Flux linked in one coil due to current in another coil.

2. Self Inductance (L)

Flux linked in a coil due to its own current.

Mutual Inductance

The flux produced in coil-2 due to the magnetic field of coil-1 (\(\Phi_{12}\)) is proportional to the current in coil-1 (\(I_1\)):

Flux linkage
\[\Phi_{12} \propto I_1 \implies \Phi_{12} = M_{12} I_1\]

where \(M_{12}\) is the mutual inductance between coil-1 and coil-2 due to current in coil-1.

If there are \(N_2\) turns in the second coil:

\[M_{12} = N_2 \frac{\Phi_{12}}{I_1} = \frac{\int_{\text{coil 2}} \vec{B}_1 \cdot d\vec{s}}{I_1}\]

The induced EMF in coil-2 due to changing current in coil-1:

⭐ Induced EMF in coil-2
\[\varepsilon_{12} = -N_2 \frac{d\Phi_{12}}{dt} = -\frac{d(M_{12}I_1)}{dt} = -M_{12}\frac{dI_1}{dt}\]

The current in coil-2: \(I_2 = \varepsilon_{12}/R_2\)

Reciprocity Theorem

Similarly, \(M_{21}\) (flux in coil-1 due to current in coil-2):

\[M_{21} = N_1 \frac{\Phi_{21}}{I_2} = \frac{\int_{\text{coil 1}} \vec{B}_2 \cdot d\vec{s}}{I_2}\]
⭐ Reciprocity Theorem
\[\boxed{M_{21} = M_{12} = M}\]

Mutual Inductance of a Loop Placed Near a Wire

  • \(M_{12}\) is independent of the current flowing through the wire.
  • It depends only on the geometry of the wire and loop.

Using Ampere's law, the magnetic field due to conductor 1 is \(\vec{B}_1 = \frac{\mu_0 I_1}{2\pi r}\):

⭐ Mutual Inductance formula
\[\boxed{M_{12} = \frac{\mu_0 h}{2\pi} \ln\!\left(\frac{s+w}{s}\right)}\]

Self Inductance

If the current in a loop is varying, an induced EMF will arise to oppose the change (Lenz's law).

  • If \(dI/dt > 0\) (current increasing), the induced current flows clockwise (opposing the increase).
  • If \(dI/dt < 0\) (current decreasing), the induced current flows counter-clockwise (opposing the decrease).
Self Inductance
The property of the loop in which its own magnetic field opposes any change in current is called self-inductance. The EMF generated is called the self-induced EMF or back EMF.
\[\Phi \propto I \implies \Phi = LI \implies L = \frac{\Phi}{I}\]
Self Inductance from magnetic field
\[L = \frac{\int_s \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S}}{I}\]
⭐ Self-induced EMF
\[\varepsilon = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = -L\frac{dI}{dt}\]

Self Inductance of a Solenoid

For a solenoid with \(N\) turns, radius \(R\), length \(h\), and turn density \(n = N/h\):

Magnetic field inside solenoid
\[B = \mu_0 n I\]
Derivation
\[L = \frac{\Phi_{total}}{I} = \frac{N\Phi_{turn}}{I} = \frac{NBA}{I} = N\mu_0 \pi n R^2\]
⭐ Self Inductance — Solenoid
\[\boxed{L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 \pi R^2}{h}}\]

Self Inductance of a Toroid

For a toroid with \(N\) turns, height \(h\), inner radius \(a\), outer radius \(b\):

Magnetic field inside toroid (from Ampere's law)
\[\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0 N I}{2\pi r}\]
Flux through a single turn
\[\Phi_{turn} = \oint_s \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S} = \frac{\mu_0 N I h}{2\pi} \ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)\]
\[L = \frac{\Phi_{total}}{I} = \frac{N\Phi_{turn}}{I} = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 I h}{2\pi I}\ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)\]
⭐ Self Inductance — Toroid
\[\boxed{L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 h}{2\pi} \ln\!\left(\frac{b}{a}\right)}\]
✅ Note: The self inductance is proportional to \(N^2\).

Magnetic Energy Density

The amount of work done to increase the current in a solenoid to a certain value is stored as magnetic energy:

\[W = \frac{1}{2}LI^2\]

The magnetic field inside the solenoid is \(B = \mu_0 n I\). Substituting \(I = B/(\mu_0 n)\) and \(L = \mu_0 N^2 \pi R^2/h\):

\[W = \frac{1}{2}L\cdot\frac{B^2 h^2}{\mu_0^2 N^2} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{\mu_0 N^2 \pi R^2}{h}\cdot\frac{B^2 h^2}{\mu_0^2 N^2}\]
⭐ Energy stored in solenoid
\[\boxed{W = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2 (h\pi R^2) = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2 \cdot V_{volume}}\]

This gives us the concept of magnetic energy density:

⭐ Magnetic Energy Density
\[\boxed{\vartheta_{mag} = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}}\]

For a non-uniform magnetic field (like a toroid), the total energy is:

\[U_{stored} = \int_{object} \vartheta_{mag} \, dV\]

Inductance from Energy Density

Equating total magnetic energy to \(\frac{1}{2}LI^2\):

\[U = \int_{vol} \vartheta_B \, dv = \frac{1}{2}LI^2\]
⭐ Alternative formula for L
\[\boxed{L = \frac{2U}{I^2}}\]

Verification — Solenoid via Energy Method

\[U_{stored} = \frac{(\mu_0 n I)^2}{2\mu_0}(\pi R^2 h) = \frac{\mu_0 n^2 I^2}{2}(\pi R^2 h)\]
\[L = \frac{2U}{I^2} = \frac{2 \cdot \mu_0(N/h)^2 I^2}{2I^2}(\pi R^2 h) = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 \pi R^2}{h} \checkmark\]

Magnetic Materials

Magnetic Dipoles

A bar magnet or a filamentary current loop is usually referred to as a magnetic dipole. When placed in a magnetic field, it experiences a torque:

\[\vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu} \times \vec{B}\]

where \(\vec{\mu}\) is the magnetic dipole moment. Examples include the Earth's magnetic field (as a dipole) and the magnetic field of the human heart.

Magnetization in Materials

In the atomic model, an electron orbits about a positive nucleus. In each atom:

  • The orbital motion of electrons around the nucleus produces a magnetic moment (like a current loop).
  • The spin motion of electrons about their own axes also produces a magnetic moment.
  • Both these motions produce magnetic fields similar to those produced by a current loop.

The magnetic moment of a current loop: \(\mu = I_b A \hat{a}_n\) where \(A\) is area of loop and \(I_b\) is bound current.

Without external field: The sum of dipole moments becomes zero due to random orientations.
With external field: Magnetic moments of electrons align themselves with the external field, producing a net non-zero magnetization.

Magnetization Vector M

If there are N atoms in a given volume \(\Delta V\) and the \(k^{th}\) atom has magnetic moment \(m_k\):

\[\mathbf{M} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{N} m_k}{\Delta V} \quad [\text{A/m}]\]

The vector M is analogous to the polarization P in dielectrics and is called the magnetic polarization density of the medium.

Bound current density
\[J_b = \nabla \times \mathbf{M}\]

For materials with \(\mathbf{M} \neq \mathbf{0}\):

\[\nabla \times \frac{\mathbf{B}}{\mu_0} = J_f + J_b = J\]
\[\nabla \times \frac{\mathbf{B}}{\mu_0} = \nabla \times \mathbf{H} + \nabla \times \mathbf{M}\]
⭐ B, H, M relation
\[\boxed{\mathbf{B} = \mu_0 (\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M})}\]

Magnetic Susceptibility and Permeability

For linear materials, \(\mathbf{M}\) depends linearly on \(\mathbf{H}\):

\[\mathbf{M} = \chi_m \mathbf{H}\]

where \(\chi_m\) is the magnetic susceptibility (dimensionless) — a measure of how sensitive the material is to a magnetic field.

\[\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(1+\chi_m)\mathbf{H} = \mu\mathbf{H} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mu_r \mathbf{H}\]
⭐ Relative Permeability
\[\mu_r = 1 + \chi_m = \frac{\mu}{\mu_0}\] where \(\mu = \mu_0 \mu_r\) is the permeability of the material (Henry/meter).

Classification of Magnetic Materials

Materials are classified by their magnetic susceptibility \(\chi_m\) or relative permeability \(\mu_r\):

Diamagnetic

\[\chi_m < 0, \quad \mu_r \leq 1\]

When exposed to a magnetic field, these materials partially oppose the external field. On an atomic scale, an EMF is induced that opposes the applied field. The magnetic field inside is smaller than the external field.

Examples: Bismuth, Copper, Water, Gold

Paramagnetic

\[\chi_m > 0, \quad \mu_r \geq 1\]

Molecules behave as dipoles. Under an external field, atomic magnets try to align with the field. When the field is removed, the net field returns to zero (no permanent magnetism). There is a force of attraction. Temperature dependent.

Examples: Aluminum, Platinum, Oxygen

Ferromagnetic

\[\chi_m \gg 0, \quad \mu_r \gg 1\]

Atoms have aligned permanent dipoles in domains. Domains align with applied field. After field removal, some domains stay aligned → permanent magnet. Very high attractive force. Temperature dependent (Curie temperature).

Examples: Iron, Nickel, Cobalt, Gadolinium

Distinct Properties of Ferromagnetic Materials

  • They are capable of being magnetized very strongly by a magnetic field.
  • They retain a considerable amount of their magnetization when removed from the field.
  • They lose their ferromagnetic properties when the temperature is raised above the Curie temperature.
  • They are nonlinear — the relation \(\mathbf{B} = \mu\mathbf{H}\) does not hold.
  • Once the field is removed, not all domains return to random positions → some domains remain aligned → material becomes a permanent magnet.
  • To remove permanent magnetism: apply mechanical force to disorient domains, or heat above Curie temperature.

B-H Curve and Hysteresis

The B-H curve (or magnetization curve) shows the relationship between the magnetic flux density \(B\) and the magnetic field intensity \(H\) for ferromagnetic materials.

Key Points on the B-H Curve

  • oa — Initial magnetization curve: Starting from the demagnetized state.
  • Hysteresis: B lags behind H (Greek: "to lag behind").
  • ob — Residual flux density: B remaining when H is reduced to zero.
  • of — Coercive field: Reverse H needed to bring B back to zero.
  • Loop area — Hysteresis loss: Energy dissipated as heat per cycle.

Practical Significance

  • Hard magnetic materials (large loop): Used for permanent magnets.
  • Soft magnetic materials (narrow loop): Used for transformer cores (low hysteresis loss).
  • Silicon iron and cast steel have different B-H characteristics.
  • Non-linearity means \(B = \mu H\) does not hold for ferromagnetic materials.

Shielding of Magnetic Fields

For perfect screening, the shield must have infinite permeability so that the applied external field does not penetrate the shield.

🧲

Magnet in Free Space

Field lines spread uniformly in all directions.

🧱

Steel Wall

Field lines are redirected through the high-permeability wall. Partial shielding.

📦

Steel Enclosure

Field lines are completely contained. Interior is shielded — near-zero field inside.

Boundary Conditions

At the interface between two magnetic media with permeabilities \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\):

Normal Components (Gauss's Law for B)

Applying \(\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{s} = 0\) to a Gaussian pillbox:

\[B_{1n}\Delta S - B_{2n}\Delta S = 0\]
⭐ Normal component continuity
\[\boxed{B_{1n} = B_{2n}} \qquad \boxed{\mu_1 H_{1n} = \mu_2 H_{2n}}\]

Normal component of B is continuous; normal component of H is discontinuous at the boundary.

Tangential Components (Ampere's Law)

Applying \(\oint \vec{H} \cdot d\vec{l} = I\) to an Amperian loop (if surface current \(K=0\)):

⭐ Tangential component continuity (K=0)
\[\boxed{H_{1t} = H_{2t}} \qquad \boxed{\frac{B_{1t}}{\mu_1} = \frac{B_{2t}}{\mu_2}}\]

Tangential component of H is continuous; tangential component of B is discontinuous.

Law of Refraction for Magnetic Flux Lines

\[\boxed{\frac{\tan\theta_1}{\tan\theta_2} = \frac{\mu_1}{\mu_2}}\]

General tangential boundary condition (with surface current density \(K\)):

\[H_{1t} - H_{2t} = K \qquad \frac{B_{1t}}{\mu_1} - \frac{B_{2t}}{\mu_2} = K\]

Magnetic Circuits

The concept of a magnetic circuit is based on solving magnetic field problems using a circuit approach. Magnetic devices such as toroids, transformers, motors, generators, and relays may be considered as magnetic circuits. There exists a strong analogy between electric and magnetic circuits.

Magnetomotive Force (MMF)

\[\mathcal{F} = NI = \oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} \quad [\text{Ampere-turns}]\]

The source of MMF in a magnetic circuit is usually a coil carrying current.

Reluctance

\[\mathfrak{R} = \frac{l}{\mu S} \quad \left[\frac{\text{A·turns}}{\text{Wb}} = \frac{1}{\text{H}}\right]\]

where \(l\) and \(S\) are mean length and cross-sectional area. The reciprocal is permeance \(\mathcal{P} = 1/\mathfrak{R}\) (Henry).

⭐ Magnetic Ohm's Law
\[\mathcal{F} = \Psi \mathfrak{R}\]

Series and Parallel Magnetic Circuits

Series Circuit (n elements)

\[\Psi_1 = \Psi_2 = \cdots = \Psi_n\] \[\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{F}_1 + \mathcal{F}_2 + \cdots + \mathcal{F}_n\]

Parallel Circuit (n branches)

\[\Psi = \Psi_1 + \Psi_2 + \cdots + \Psi_n\] \[\mathcal{F}_1 = \mathcal{F}_2 = \cdots = \mathcal{F}_n\]

Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell's Equations — State Before Modification

Before Maxwell's correction, the four equations were:

Gauss's Law (Electric)
\[\oint_s \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}\]
Faraday's Law
\[\oint_\ell \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = \int_s \left(-\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\right) \cdot d\vec{s}\]
Gauss's Law (Magnetic)
\[\oint_s \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{s} = 0\]
Ampere's Law (Original)
\[\oint_\ell \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enclosed}\]

Displacement Current — Maxwell's Correction

⚠ Inconsistency in Ampere's Law Consider a capacitor being charged by a current \(I\). For surface \(S_1\) (flat disc): \(I_{enclosed} = I\). For surface \(S_2\) (bag/balloon shape that passes between capacitor plates): \(I_{enclosed} = 0\). The result of Ampere's law depends on which surface we choose — there is an ambiguity! Maxwell found this inconsistency and fixed it!

Maxwell's Resolution

1
When current is increasing, the capacitor is charging, and the electric field between the plates is increasing (\(dE/dt > 0\)).
2
In surface \(S_2\), there should be a term with units of Ampere that relates to the increasing \(E\): \[\oint_\ell \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0(I_{enclosed} + \ldots)\]
3
The electric field between the plates: \(|\vec{E}| = Q/(\varepsilon_0 A)\), so \(\frac{dQ}{dt} = \frac{d\vec{E}}{dt}\varepsilon_0 A = \frac{d\vec{D}}{dt}A\)
4
The rate of change of charge is current — this is the displacement current: \[I_{dis} = \frac{dQ}{dt} = \frac{d\vec{D}}{dt}A\]
⭐ Displacement Current Density
\[\boxed{J_d = \frac{\partial\mathbf{D}}{\partial t}}\]
⭐ Maxwell-Ampere Equation
\[\oint_\ell \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0(I_{enclosed} + I_{displacement})\]

Rigorous Derivation of Displacement Current

In the point (differential) form of Ampere's law:

\[\nabla \times H = J\]

From vector identity, the divergence of a curl is always zero:

\[\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times H) = 0 = \nabla \cdot J\]

But from the continuity equation:

\[\nabla \cdot J = -\frac{\partial \rho_v}{\partial t} \neq 0\]

There is a contradiction! Maxwell corrected this by adding \(J_d\):

\[\nabla \times H = J + J_d\] \[\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times H) = \nabla \cdot (J + J_d) = 0\] \[\nabla \cdot J_d = \frac{\partial \rho_v}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot D) = \nabla \cdot \frac{\partial D}{\partial t}\]
⭐ Therefore
\[\boxed{J_d = \frac{\partial D}{\partial t}}\]
⭐ Maxwell-Ampere Law (Point Form)
\[\boxed{\nabla \times H = J + \frac{\partial D}{\partial t}}\]

Final Form of Maxwell's Equations

🌟 The Four Laws of Electromagnetism These four equations unify electricity, magnetism, and optics. They are the foundation of classical electrodynamics.

Gauss's Law (Electric) \(\oint_s \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{s} = \dfrac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}\) \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_v\)
Faraday's Law \(\oint_\ell \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\oint_s \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{s}\) \(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\dfrac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\)
Gauss's Law (Magnetic) \(\oint_s \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{s} = 0\) \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\)
Maxwell-Ampere Law \(\oint_\ell \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0\!\left(\oint_s\!\left(\mathbf{J} + \dfrac{\partial\mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\right)\!d\mathbf{s}\right)\) \(\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \dfrac{\partial\mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\)

Maxwell's Equations — Point Form

\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_v\]
\[\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\]
\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\]
\[\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial\mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\]
✅ Physical Meaning of Each Equation:
  • \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_v\): Electric charges are sources of the electric field.
  • \(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t\): Time-varying magnetic fields create electric fields (Faraday).
  • \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\): There are no magnetic monopoles; magnetic field lines are closed loops.
  • \(\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \partial\mathbf{D}/\partial t\): Electric currents AND time-varying electric fields create magnetic fields (Maxwell-Ampere).
